All Eyes on Them: Television and American Politics

This section explores how television transformed American politics by shifting the focus from ideas to image. Before television became widespread, political communication relied mostly on speeches, newspapers, and radio. In those formats, a candidate's message and voice mattered more than how they looked. Television changed that by adding a visual element, which affected how candidates presented themselves and how voters judged them.

As television became more common in the 1950s and 1960s, it began to reshape political campaigns. Candidates started using televised advertisements and appearances to reach voters directly in their homes. Political events such as debates and election coverage also became something people could watch in real time. This made politics feel more immediate and turned it into a shared viewing experience.

These changes made politics more accessible, but they also introduced new challenges. Voters began to rely more on visual impressions, such as a candidate's appearance, confidence, and demeanor. This meant that success in politics was no longer based only on ideas, but also on how well a candidate performed on screen.

By examining these developments through a series of artifacts, this section shows how television changed what voters paid attention to and how political campaigns were run.

Television transformed elections into live events. Coverage like this allowed viewers to follow results in real time, shifting politics from something read about after the fact to something experienced as it unfolded.

The 1960 debate was one of the first major political events shaped by television.

Television changed how voters evaluated candidates. A study comparing radio and television audiences of the 1960 debate show that viewers were more influenced by visual impressions, such as appearance and demeanor. The study concludes that Kennedy's strong on screen image may have given him an advantage, demonstrating how television shifted political judgment toward image and personality (Druckman 2003).

Campaign events like “Kennedy Teas” were designed to build a relatable public image. These appearances emphasized personality and personal connection, reflecting the growing importance of image in political campaigning.

Television ads allowed candidates to deliver messages directly to voters in their homes.

Television required candidates to adapt their campaign to a visual medium. As Jamieson notes, candidates like Eisenhowers opponent, Adlai Stevenson, struggled to appear natural on camera, while Eisenhower's televised presence reinforced his public image of a decisive, dependable veteran. Success in politics began depending more on performance and visual presentation (Jamieson 1988).

Television enabled campaigns to use emotion as a persuasive tool. The “Daisy” ad relied on fear and imagery rather than detailed policy, demonstrating how television could more effectively influence voters through emotional appeal.

The 1964 "Daisy" ad was a part of Lyndon B. Johnson's presidential campaign. The ad does not directly discuss policy changes Johnson intends to make as president, instead, it relies on instilling fear into an audience. This method of campaigning, made easier by the television as visuals create more emotional impact, reflects how television changed how candidates got messages through to their voters.
