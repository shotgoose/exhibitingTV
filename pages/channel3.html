All Eyes on Them: Television and American Politics

This section explores how television transformed American politics by shifting the focus from ideas to image. Before the rise of television, political communication relied primarily on speeches, print media, and radio, where a candidate's voice and message carried more weight than their physical appearance. Television introduced a new visual dimension to politics, changing how candidates presented themselves and how voters evaluated them.

As television became more widespread in the 1950s and 1960s, it reshaped both political campaigns and public engagement. Campaigns relied on televised advertisements and appearances to reach voters, encouraging candidates to craft a carefully controlled public image. At the same time, political events such as debates and election coverage became shared viewing experiences, turning politics into something that could be watched and interpreted in real time.

This shift brought new opportunities but also new challenges. Television made politics more accessible to a broader audience, but it also encouraged voters to rely more heavily on visual impressions, such as appearance, demeanor, and emotional appeal. By examining these changes through a series of artifacts, this section highlights how television altered what mattered to voters in political decision making.

Television transformed elections into live events. Coverage like this allowed viewers to follow results in real time, shifting politics from something read about after the fact to something experienced as it unfolded.

The 1960 debate was one of the first major political events shaped by television.

Television changed how voters evaluated candidates. A study comparing radio and television audiences of the 1960 debate show that viewers were more influenced by visual impressions, such as appearance and demeanor. The study concludes that Kennedy's strong on screen image may have given him an advantage, demonstrating how television shifted political judgment toward image and personality (Druckman 2003).

Campaign events like “Kennedy Teas” were designed to build a relatable public image. These appearances emphasized personality and personal connection, reflecting the growing importance of image in political campaigning.

Television ads allowed candidates to deliver messages directly to voters in their homes.

Television required candidates to adapt their campaign to a visual medium. As Jamieson notes, candidates like Eisenhowers opponent, Adlai Stevenson, struggled to appear natural on camera, while Eisenhower's televised presence reinforced his public image of a decisive, dependable veteran. Success in politics began depending more on performance and visual presentation (Jamieson 1988).

Television enabled campaigns to use emotion as a persuasive tool. The “Daisy” ad relied on fear and imagery rather than detailed policy, demonstrating how television could more effectively influence voters through emotional appeal.
