Exhibiting Technology: The Television

Roan Fijen

This digital exhibit explores the role of the television in the United States during the twentieth century as a force that reshaped how Americans communicated and spent their time. Television, among other technologies, is often discussed in terms of its technological developments and milestones, but that approach does not depict its impact on society. Instead of focusing on a timeline of "firsts," this exhibit looks at how television changed the lives of Americans.

By the mid-twentieth century, television had gone from being a new and unfamiliar device to something found in a majority of American homes. This shift happened quickly, and because of that, television became embedded in daily routines before people had fully adjusted to it. Unlike earlier forms of media like newspapers or radio, television required people to sit down and pay attention to a screen. This had a noticeable impact on how people spent their time and interacted with others.

Television also created shared experiences on a much larger scale. People across the country could watch the same programs at the same time, which helped create a more unified popular culture. At the same time, this kind of shared viewing came with tradeoffs. Time spent watching television often replaced other activities, including social interaction, hobbies, and community involvement. Because of this, television can be seen as one of the first major technologies that began isolating people in a major way.

This exhibit is organized around two main perspectives that help explain these changes. The first looks at television in the context of daily life, focusing on how it affected routines, social interaction, and entertainment. The second looks at television in politics, focusing on how it changed campaigns and how voters perceived candidates. These two perspectives are meant to show that television was not limited to one area of life, but instead had a wide range of effects.

The first section, "Life Around the Screen", approaches television from a domestic and social perspective. This section focuses on how television changed the way Americans spent their time, interacted with one another, and engaged with entertainment. It also considers how television influenced the structure of daily routines and the layout of the home, where living rooms increasingly became organized around the screen. By looking at these kinds of everyday changes, the section shows how television became a normal and expected part of daily life.

The second section, "All Eyes on Them", examines television as a tool of political communication and influence. This section focuses on how television changed the way political campaigns were run and how voters perceived their candidates. Rather than simply relaying information, television introduced a visual aspect to politics, where the appearance and charisma of political candidates became increasingly important. It also made political events more immediate, allowing people to watch debates and election coverage as they happened instead of reading about them later.

Taken together, these sections show that television was more than just a new form of entertainment. It changed how people organized their time, how they interacted with others, and how they understood public events. By looking at both everyday life and politics, this exhibit shows how television became a central part of American culture during the twentieth century.
